Learning Experience Design

My practice is built on a single conviction: learning is not the transfer of information. It is the reorganization of understanding. Every design decision I make is grounded in that premise — and in the evidence of what actually enables it.

Theoretical foundations

Where the practice comes from

My approach draws from three intersecting disciplines: fine art education and studio pedagogy, neurobiology of integrated learning, and instructional systems design. These are not separate influences — they are in constant conversation in how I design.

🎨

Studio pedagogy & experiential learning

Years working directly with students in studios and shops taught me that the deepest learning happens through making, failing, iterating, and getting feedback — not through instruction alone. Kolb's experiential learning cycle describes this formally; studios practice it instinctively.

Kolb, D. A. (1984). Experiential learning. Prentice Hall.
🧠

Neurobiology of integrated learning

Research into how integrated, learner-centered engagement supports cognitive health outcomes points consistently toward the same design conditions: relevance, agency, social scaffolding, and reflection. Learning that feels meaningful is learning that lasts.

Immordino-Yang, M. H. & Damasio, A. (2007). We feel, therefore we learn. Mind, Brain, and Education, 1(1).
⚙️

Learner-centered instructional design

The role of the designer is not to deliver content — it is to architect conditions. This means working backward from observable, measurable outcomes; anticipating where learners are likely to get stuck; and building formative checkpoints that surface understanding before it calcifies into misunderstanding.

Wiggins, G. & McTighe, J. (2005). Understanding by design. ASCD.

The learning rhythm

Five phases, one movement

Each learning experience I design moves through the same rhythm. The sequence is not arbitrary — it mirrors how the brain builds and consolidates understanding, and how the body builds skill. I start where the learner already is, and I end where they can take what they've learned somewhere it hasn't been yet.

Why Relevance & activation

Before any new knowledge is introduced, learners need a reason to want it. This phase surfaces existing knowledge, connects to the learner's real context, and establishes why this matters — not in the abstract, but in their specific circumstances.

Grounded in prior knowledge activation research and self-determination theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000), which identifies relevance and autonomy as foundational to intrinsic motivation.

See Concept-building

New information is introduced through demonstrations, models, worked examples, and visual scaffolding. This phase builds on what learners already know rather than assuming a blank slate, using concrete anchors before moving to abstraction.

Aligned with cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) and Mayer's principles of multimedia learning, which show that well-scaffolded examples reduce extraneous load and improve schema formation.

Try Active & experiential

Learners engage directly with the material through activities, exercises, or real-world applications. Mistakes here are design features, not failures — they generate feedback that accelerates understanding more effectively than passive review.

Supported by research on retrieval practice and desirable difficulties (Bjork, 2011), as well as Dewey's foundational work on learning through structured experience.

Share Social learning

Learning consolidates in community. This phase creates structured opportunities for discussion, peer feedback, and collaborative sense-making. Social scaffolding also serves a function that cognitive design alone cannot: it addresses the social determinants that shape whether learning sticks.

Rooted in Vygotsky's zone of proximal development and Wenger's communities of practice, as well as research linking social determinants of health to learning outcomes.

Reflect Integration & transfer

The final phase is where learners take ownership of what they've built. Structured reflection prompts the metacognitive work that converts temporary understanding into durable knowledge — and enables transfer to novel contexts.

Supported by metacognitive research (Flavell, 1979; Bransford et al., 2000, How People Learn) linking reflective practice to long-term retention and adaptive expertise.

Design approach

How I work with subject matter experts

Learning design is a collaborative discipline. The best content emerges from a genuine partnership between the designer and the people who hold deep expertise in the subject — not from treating expertise as raw material to be processed.

My process is structured in three phases — assessment and analysis, design and development, evaluation and sustainment — each with clear milestones, stakeholder touchpoints, and built-in opportunities to course-correct before moving forward.

  • I work backward from outcomes, not forward from content. The first question is always: what should learners be able to do, and how will we know?
  • Assessment is built in from the beginning — both formative checkpoints throughout learning and summative measures of whether outcomes have been met.
  • Accessibility is a design constraint, not an afterthought. It shapes format, pacing, and delivery choices from the earliest stage.
  • I use a phased workflow with defined deliverables at each stage: needs assessment, research, storyboard, beta testing, quality assurance, and sustainment planning.

On evidence and design

Design decisions are only as good as the reasoning behind them. I bring two kinds of evidence to my work: the research literature that informs the structure of learning experiences, and the data generated by learners themselves — through formative feedback, completion patterns, and evaluation instruments — that shows whether the design is actually working.

Good evaluation doesn't begin at the end of a program. It's designed in from the start, at every level: learner reaction, knowledge acquisition, behavioral transfer, and organizational impact.

Assessment framework informed by Kirkpatrick's four-level model and its evidence-based extensions (Kirkpatrick & Kirkpatrick, 2016).

Workflow structure

From first conversation to sustained use

Phase 1

Assessment, Analysis & Design

Learning needs assessment, stakeholder interviews, research synthesis, project scoping, and storyboarding. This phase ends with a design milestone: a complete blueprint for the learning experience before a single piece of content is built.

Reverse-engineered from the storyboard output. Decision gates at each stage allow the team to validate assumptions early, before investment in development.
Phase 2

Development, Testing & Delivery Preparation

Content development, beta testing with real learners, quality assurance against accessibility and instructional standards, and preparation for deployment. This phase ends at the delivery milestone.

Beta testing is structured — not informal — with documented feedback and a formal QA process. Learner feedback is treated as design data, not an optional add-on.
Phase 3

Evaluation, Impact & Sustainment

Post-delivery evaluation, impact reporting, and sustainment planning. This phase ensures that the program continues to work over time — and that the organization understands what it achieved.

Designed from the perspective of the organization's long-term needs, not just the immediate learning event. Sustainment includes update triggers, maintenance cycles, and knowledge management.
